Rationale

Since May, Massachusetts has used a definition for COVID-19 risk level based on the daily incidence by population over the previous two weeks. It was a reasonable system, if not used effectively.

Governor Charlie Baker made clear that his primary goal is to keep the economy going and schools open. Not suppressing spread of the virus.

So what does he do when cases start to explode? Change the risk levels.

Under the new system, every community that was previously medium risk and just barely high risk was now "low risk".

Under the new system, every community that was previously solidly high risk was deemed "medium risk" so long as their testing positivity was low enough.

And for some reason, the thresholds were adjusted slightly for three population categories. Even though incidence is already expressed on a per-population basis. And something about not wanting to "penalize" cities for testing more?

(I watched the press conference revealing the new system, Gov. Baker couldn't clearly articulate the rationale for any of this.)

That said, he gave away the real rationale: Moving the goal posts to keep schools open.

As a result, I started generating maps using the old system, with some tweaks to reveal communities with 0 cases, and added additional shades of red to show off increases within the high risk category. Code (in Julia) is available on GitHub.

The maps are updated after the Massachusetts Weekly Public Health Report is released each week.

I also maintain a graph of the incidence rate in Boston neighborhoods and an overview of COVID behavior across the US.

Boston COVID-19 Weekly Incidence

Data from the Boston Public Health Commission weekly report, which is usually released on Fridays. Massachusetts reopening stages are marked. Second axis goes along with the "Overall" dashed line. Some cases aren't assigned to a neighborhood, which is why the Boston line sometimes departs from the stack.

Isn't it odd how we were doing great until we started reopening?
